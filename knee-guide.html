Understanding Your Knee
Knee Anatomy
What's actually inside your knee
Your knee is the largest joint in your body and takes roughly 3–4x your bodyweight with every running step. Understanding what's in there helps you understand why certain exercises work.
Patella (kneecap) — floats in front of the joint, held in a groove by the surrounding muscles. When those muscles are weak or imbalanced, it tracks incorrectly — causing pain.
Cartilage (meniscus) — two C-shaped pads that cushion the joint. They absorb impact and need strong surrounding muscles to avoid taking too much load.
Ligaments — tough bands holding the bones together. The ACL, PCL, MCL, and LCL provide stability in all directions.
Patellar tendon — connects the kneecap to the tibia. Carries massive force during running and jumping. Very susceptible to overuse.
The Condition
Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome)
Runner's knee is pain around or behind the kneecap, caused by the patella not tracking correctly in its groove. Instead of gliding smoothly, it grinds against the bone underneath. The root cause is almost never the knee itself — it's weak glutes and hips forcing the kneecap off its normal path.

The good news: it responds extremely well to the right exercises. Most people see significant improvement within 4–6 weeks of consistent strengthening.

⚠️
When to see a doctor instead
If you have sudden severe pain, significant swelling, inability to bear weight, a popping sensation during injury, or pain that hasn't improved after 6 weeks of these exercises — see a physiotherapist. This guide is for chronic dull pain, not acute injuries.
Runner's Knee Exercises
Glutes Hip abductors
Clamshells
3 sets · 15 reps each side · Rest 45 sec
The most important runner's knee exercise. Directly targets the gluteus medius — the muscle responsible for keeping your kneecap tracking correctly. Weak glute meds are the single biggest cause of patellofemoral pain.
How to do it
1
Lie on your side with your hips stacked and knees bent to about 45 degrees. Keep your feet together throughout the whole movement.
2
Keeping your feet touching, rotate your top knee upward toward the ceiling as far as you can without your hips rolling back.
3
Hold at the top for 2 seconds, then lower slowly. The lowering is as important as the lifting.
4
Complete all reps on one side before switching.
Make it harder: Place a resistance band just above your knees. Make it easier: reduce range of motion until you build strength.
Quads Glutes
Step-downs
3 sets · 12 reps each leg · Rest 60 sec
Directly retrains how your kneecap tracks during the exact motion that causes pain — controlled downward movement. This is one of the most functional exercises for runner's knee because it mimics running's loading pattern.
How to do it
1
Stand on a step or a sturdy box with one foot. The other foot hangs off the edge.
2
Slowly bend the standing knee, lowering the hanging foot toward the floor. Count 3–4 seconds going down. Don't let the knee cave inward.
3
Tap the floor lightly (or stop just before) then return to standing using only the standing leg.
4
Watch your knee in a mirror — it should stay directly over your second toe, never diving inward.
Key form cue: The moment your knee collapses inward is your glute med failing. Stop and reset. Quality over quantity every time.
Piriformis Outer hip
Figure-four stretch
Hold 45 seconds · 3 rounds each side · Daily
Releases the piriformis and outer hip — tight muscles that rotate the femur inward and put abnormal stress on the kneecap. This stretch is often the fastest way to get immediate pain relief from runner's knee.
How to do it
1
Lie on your back with both knees bent and feet flat on the floor.
2
Cross your right ankle over your left knee, letting the right knee fall open to the side.
3
Reach through the gap and pull both legs toward your chest until you feel a deep stretch in the right outer glute and hip.
4
Hold, breathe deeply, and let gravity pull the knee further open with each exhale.
Where you should feel it: Deep in the outer glute of the crossed leg — not in the knee itself. If you feel it in the knee, check your foot position.
Quads Glutes
Wall Sit
3 sets · 30–45 seconds · Rest 60 sec
Builds quad endurance at the exact angle where runner's knee tends to flare up. Unlike squats, the wall removes balance from the equation so you can focus entirely on muscle activation and holding the position correctly.
How to do it
1
Stand with your back flat against a wall. Walk your feet out until your thighs are parallel to the floor — or as close as you can manage.
2
Your knees should be at about 90 degrees and directly above your ankles — not in front of them.
3
Press your lower back into the wall. Don't let it arch away.
4
Hold for the target time. When it gets easy, hold longer before adding a second set.
Pain warning: If you feel sharp pain in the kneecap during this, raise your body slightly up the wall so the knee angle is less severe. Build the range gradually.
General Knee Strengthening
Glutes Hamstrings
Glute Bridge
3 sets · 20 reps · Hold 2 sec at top · Rest 45 sec
The foundation of knee health. Strong glutes stabilise the femur (thigh bone) and prevent the inward collapse that causes kneecap grinding. This is the exercise that should never leave your routine — even after your knee feels completely better.
How to do it
1
Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor about hip-width apart. Arms flat at your sides.
2
Push through your heels and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
3
Squeeze your glutes hard at the top. Hold for 2 seconds. You should feel it entirely in your glutes, not your lower back.
4
Lower slowly and repeat without letting your hips fully drop to the floor between reps.
Progress to: Single-leg glute bridge — same movement but one foot lifted. Massively harder and much more specific to running mechanics.
Quads Glutes Hip stabilisers
Slow Eccentric Squat
3 sets · 10 reps · 4 seconds down · Rest 60 sec
The eccentric (lowering) phase of a squat is where the knee takes the most stress — and also where it gets the most benefit from training. Going down slowly builds incredible quad and glute strength while teaching the knee to absorb load correctly.
How to do it
1
Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes turned out slightly (about 15–30 degrees).
2
Take 4 full seconds to lower into the squat. Count out loud if it helps — most people rush this part.
3
Lower until your thighs are parallel to the floor, or as far as is comfortable without knee pain.
4
Stand back up at normal speed. The slow part is the going down, not the coming up.
Knee tracking: Your knees must travel in the same direction as your toes. If they cave inward, widen your stance or add a resistance band above the knees for feedback.
Quads Glutes
Reverse Lunge
3 sets · 10 reps each leg · Rest 60 sec
Much easier on the knee than a forward lunge because the shin stays more vertical, reducing shear force on the kneecap. Builds the exact muscles needed for running while being safe to do even during a runner's knee flare-up.
How to do it
1
Stand tall with feet together. Step one foot backward, landing on the ball of that foot.
2
Lower your back knee toward the floor — aim to hover about an inch above it without touching.
3
Your front shin should stay roughly vertical. Your front knee tracks directly over your second toe.
4
Push through the front heel to stand back up. Complete all reps on one leg before switching.
Use a wall: Place one hand on a wall for balance until you're confident with the movement. Perfect form matters more than reps.

What to expect
Your 4-week progress path
Print this page and check off your sessions as you go. Most people feel significant improvement by week 3.
W1
Foundation
Learn the movements. Focus on form not reps. Pain during exercises is okay — sharp pain means stop.
W2
Building
Add reps. Start to feel the glutes engaging. Pain during daily activities should start to reduce.
W3
Adapting
Morning stiffness improves. Stairs feel easier. Try the single-leg glute bridge progression.
W4
Stronger
Most pain should be significantly reduced. Don't stop here — keep going to make these gains permanent.
